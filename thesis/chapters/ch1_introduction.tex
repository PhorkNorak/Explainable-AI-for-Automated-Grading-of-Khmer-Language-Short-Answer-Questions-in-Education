\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

\section{Background to the Study}
Assessment is a core part of education. It measures what students have learned, guides teaching, and
gives learners feedback on how to improve. Much of this assessment uses \emph{short-answer questions}:
a student writes one to a few sentences, which are then judged for \emph{content correctness} against
a reference answer. Short-answer questions sit between multiple-choice items (easy to mark but mostly
testing recall) and essays (rich but slow to grade). They are common in school exams, and grading
them by hand is slow and not always consistent, which limits how often teachers can give detailed
feedback.

\emph{Automatic Short-Answer Grading} (ASAG) studies how to score these answers with a computer. The
field has moved from early similarity-based systems to deep neural networks, and most recently to
Transformer-based and large language models~\citep{sung2022survey,devlin2019bert}. These advances have
improved ASAG for high-resource languages such as English, Arabic, and Chinese.

Those benefits have mostly \emph{not} reached low-resource languages. \textbf{Khmer}
(\km{ភាសាខ្មែរ}), the official language of Cambodia and spoken by more than 17 million people, is
still low-resource in NLP. The Khmer script is also hard to process:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item it has \textbf{no spaces between words}, so even splitting a sentence into words is a
  machine-learning task in itself;
  \item it stacks subscript consonants (\km{ជើង}) and reorders vowels inside a syllable;
  \item it carries invisible formatting characters that survive naive cleaning.
\end{itemize}

Multilingual models see little Khmer during pre-training, and existing Khmer NLP work covers
tokenisation, part-of-speech tagging, and search, but not grading~\citep{buoy2022khmer,khmernltk2020}.
At the same time, AI in education is a growing priority for Cambodia~\citep{huot2025cambodia}, which
makes a reliable, transparent Khmer grading tool both timely and useful.

\section{Problem Statement}
We want to grade Khmer short answers automatically and to \emph{honestly} describe what is achievable
on a real classroom corpus. Three properties of the data make this hard:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{No benchmark and no baselines.} There is no published Khmer ASAG dataset or system, so
  we must build both the benchmark and the reference results.
  \item \textbf{Different scoring scales.} Each question has its own maximum score, from the set
  $\{5,6,7,8,10,12,15,20\}$. A raw score of ``8'' means different things on different questions, so the
  scores must be normalised.
  \item \textbf{A small, single-grader corpus.} The corpus has 1{,}184 answers graded by \emph{one}
  teacher, so there is limited data, no second grader to measure a human ceiling, and some real
  marking noise to confront.
\end{itemize}

A further problem, often blurred in ASAG papers, is that two different questions are usually reported
together: \emph{research quality} (how well the model's grades agree with the teacher, measured by
QWK) and \emph{deployment quality} (whether the model predicts the \emph{exact} mark in points). We
keep the two apart, because a model can be good at one and weak at the other. Finally, with only 41
distinct questions, a model can memorise question-specific patterns, so we must also test it on
\emph{unseen} questions.

\section{Aim and Objectives of the Study}
The aim of this study is to build the first reproducible, \emph{explainable} ASAG benchmark for Khmer
and to describe honestly what current models can and cannot do on it. To reach this aim, our
objectives are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.8em]
  \item to build and document a Khmer short-answer corpus with three cleaning variants and a
  Khmer-aware preprocessing pipeline;
  \item to implement one shared training-and-evaluation pipeline that compares four model families
  (classical, RNN, Transformer encoder, and a fine-tuned LLM) as bounded ordinal regression;
  \item to design a model-agnostic explainability protocol that \emph{measures} explanation
  faithfulness and plausibility, and to apply it to all four families;
  \item to evaluate every model with field-standard metrics across three dataset variants, and to
  measure the question-leakage effect;
  \item to deliver the explainability feature as a live, teacher-facing web prototype.
\end{enumerate}

In meeting these objectives, the study answers six research questions:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.4em,style=nextline]
  \item[RQ1.] Does the most expensive model (a fine-tuned LLM) actually beat a 30-second CPU baseline
  on a real Khmer exam corpus?
  \item[RQ2.] On small-data ASAG, does better \emph{data quality} help more than changing the learning
  algorithm?
  \item[RQ3.] Does the model that wins QWK also win the metrics a classroom cares about (the exact
  mark, and agreement within one point)?
  \item[RQ4.] Do two cheap additions help: giving the model each question's maximum score, and
  post-hoc threshold calibration?
  \item[RQ5.] Across the four families, which gives the most \emph{faithful} explanations, and is there
  an accuracy-versus-explainability trade-off?
  \item[RQ6.] How large is the \emph{question-leakage} effect, and how well do models grade genuinely
  unseen questions?
\end{description}

\section{Rationale of the Study}
This study is motivated by a clear gap: short-answer grading has advanced for high-resource languages
but not for Khmer, and a grade is a high-stakes decision that a teacher will only trust if it can be
\emph{audited}. An explanation is only useful if it is \emph{faithful}, that is, if it reflects what
the model actually used rather than just looking reasonable; a plausible but unfaithful explanation is
arguably worse than none, because it creates false confidence~\citep{lyu2024faithfulsurvey}. We
therefore treat explainability as a first-class goal and measure whether explanations are faithful.

The study makes the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.8em]
  \item \textbf{The first Khmer ASAG benchmark}: 1{,}184 real teacher-graded answers (41 questions,
  203 students, four subjects, two schools), released with three cleaning variants.
  \item \textbf{A four-family comparison under one pipeline}: classical, RNN, Transformer, and a
  fine-tuned LLM (the KhmerGrader family).
  \item \textbf{A cross-family faithfulness protocol}: one model-agnostic Leave-One-Out (LOO)
  word-attribution method applied to all families and \emph{measured} for faithfulness.
  \item \textbf{An honest question-leakage analysis} that measures how much small-question-pool scores
  are inflated.
  \item \textbf{A live teacher-facing prototype} that returns a score, a faithful word-attribution
  explanation, and written feedback.
\end{enumerate}

To ground the data in reality, we collected real semester exam answers by hand from two Cambodian high
schools chosen to differ: one in a province and one in the capital. The two sites differ in students'
answering style and in the marking teacher, so a model that works across both is tested against real
classroom variation.

\section{Limitation and Scope}
This study covers short-answer questions in Khmer from four secondary-school subjects (Biology,
History, Geography, Earth Science). We study \emph{content} scoring against a reference answer, not
essay style or grammar. A single teacher graded every answer, so results are agreement \emph{with that
grader}, not against a measured human ceiling. The heavier models (the Transformer encoders and the
LLM) need a GPU and were run on dedicated hardware; the classical and RNN baselines run on a CPU. We
revisit these limits in Chapter~\ref{ch:limitations}.
