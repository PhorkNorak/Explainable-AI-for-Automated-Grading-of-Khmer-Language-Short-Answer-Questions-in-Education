\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:literature}

This chapter reviews the work this study builds on: an overview of automatic short-answer grading
(\S\ref{sec:lit-asag}), the challenges of Khmer (\S\ref{sec:lit-khmer}), the core concepts of
Transformers and explainability (\S\ref{sec:lit-core}), related work (\S\ref{sec:lit-related}), and
the gap in existing research (\S\ref{sec:lit-gap}).

\section{Overview of Automatic Short-Answer Grading}
\label{sec:lit-asag}
Automatic Short-Answer Grading (ASAG) scores a free-text student answer by comparing it, directly or
indirectly, to a reference answer. The field has gone through four overlapping stages:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{Similarity-based.} Early systems scored answers by how similar they were to a
  reference, using lexical and dependency features.
  \item \textbf{Shared tasks.} Standard datasets and the idea of testing on \emph{unseen questions}
  made systems comparable.
  \item \textbf{Deep learning.} Work moved from word embeddings to neural and Transformer models, with
  data augmentation to cope with small data~\citep{sung2022survey,tan2025review}.
  \item \textbf{Large language models.} LLMs are now fine-tuned or prompted for grading. Fine-tuned
  specialist models usually beat prompted general ones~\citep{chang2024gpt4}.
\end{itemize}

Throughout these stages, \emph{Quadratic Weighted Kappa} (QWK) has stayed the standard metric for
ordinal scoring, usually reported with exact and adjacent (within-one-point) agreement.

\section{Challenges in Khmer}
\label{sec:lit-khmer}
Khmer is a low-resource language whose script makes grading harder than in English:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item there are \textbf{no spaces between words}, so a system must first segment the text before any
  grading model sees it;
  \item the script stacks subscript consonants and reorders vowels inside a syllable, and it carries
  invisible characters that must be cleaned;
  \item multilingual models see little Khmer during pre-training.
\end{itemize}

Khmer NLP has focused on foundational tasks: word segmentation, part-of-speech tagging, and
pre-trained models~\citep{buoy2022khmer,khmernltk2020}. This study uses the \texttt{khmer-nltk}
segmenter for the segmentation step. More broadly, preparing Cambodian education for AI is an active
concern~\citep{huot2025cambodia}, but grading Khmer student answers is still unaddressed.

\section{Core Concepts: Transformers and Explainability}
\label{sec:lit-core}

\subsection{Transformers and Pre-trained Encoders}
The Transformer replaced recurrence with self-attention and is the backbone of modern NLP. It lets
every word attend to every other word, capturing long-range links that recurrent models reach only
with difficulty. Pre-trained encoders built on it learn general text representations that can be
fine-tuned with little task data. BERT and its multilingual version mBERT~\citep{devlin2019bert} use a
masked-language-modelling objective; cross-lingual encoders such as XLM-R scale this to many languages,
which suits Khmer's multilingual setting. BERT, mBERT, XLM-R, GTE-multilingual, and the Qwen LLM used
in this study are \emph{all} Transformers; they differ in size and how they are adapted, not in their
core design.

\subsection{Explainability and Faithfulness}
Explainable AI (XAI) makes model decisions transparent. \emph{Attribution} methods give each input
word an importance score; common ones are perturbation methods (LIME and \emph{occlusion}), gradient
methods (saliency, Integrated Gradients), and the game-theoretic SHAP. The key distinction is between
\emph{faithfulness} (does the explanation reflect what the model actually computed?) and
\emph{plausibility} (does it look reasonable to a human?)~\citep{lyu2024faithfulsurvey}. The two can
disagree, and a convincing-but-unfaithful explanation is the dangerous case. Faithfulness can be
measured with the ERASER protocol's \emph{comprehensiveness} and \emph{sufficiency}~\citep{deyoung2020eraser},
summarised across removal fractions by the area-over-the-perturbation-curve (AOPC). This study takes
that measure-don't-assume stance and compares each explanation against a random-removal baseline.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:lit-related}
In grading specifically, XAI is used both to explain scores and to give feedback. Kumar and Boulanger
showed that explainable automated essay scoring has pedagogical value, using SHAP over interpretable
features~\citep{kumar2020explainable}. Most later scoring-XAI work has also relied on SHAP, LIME, or
Integrated Gradients~\citep{lachana2025dutch}, and one study reports that SHAP and LIME are poorly
aligned with rubric assessment~\citep{xu2025qwenscore}. A systematic comparison of attribution methods
for ASAG (including leave-one-out) motivates the occlusion-style method used here~\citep{pinto2025attribution}.
For evaluation, recent work compares fine-tuned models against prompted LLMs for grading and reports
QWK with exact and adjacent agreement~\citep{lachana2025dutch}. The closest comparable dataset is an
Arabic ASAG corpus on a three-point scale, whose best Transformer reaches 95.67\,\%/77.22\,\%
train/test accuracy and an unweighted Cohen's $\kappa$ of 0.60~\citep{alaoui2024}; we use it as the
anchor for honestly reporting the train/test gap. Table~\ref{tab:priorwork} places this study among
representative recent datasets; metric values are not comparable across rows.

\section{Gap in Existing Research}
\label{sec:lit-gap}
Three gaps stand out:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.8em]
  \item there is \textbf{no published ASAG benchmark for Khmer}, so the field has no data and no
  baselines;
  \item much ASAG work \emph{shows} explanations without \emph{measuring} whether they are faithful,
  and most relies on SHAP-style methods;
  \item benchmarks with few distinct questions risk \emph{question leakage}, which inflates scores, yet
  this is rarely measured.
\end{enumerate}

This study addresses all three: it builds the first reproducible Khmer ASAG benchmark, measures
explanation faithfulness across four model families with one model-agnostic method, and measures the
question-leakage effect with a question-held-out evaluation.
