\section{Research Design}
\label{sec:design}
We use a quantitative, experiment-driven design. We frame all four model families as the same task,
\emph{bounded ordinal regression}, and train and evaluate them through one shared, Khmer-aware
pipeline, so that differences in results come from the models rather than from inconsistent
preprocessing or evaluation. Each model predicts a continuous normalised score $\hat{y}\in[0,1]$, which
we map to an integer grade for reporting. We then judge every model on two axes: standard agreement
metrics \emph{and} the plausibility of its explanations. Figure~\ref{fig:method} shows the overall
research pipeline.

\section{Data Collection}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Provenance}
We built the corpus from \emph{real} semester examination answers, not synthetic or crowd-sourced
text. We collected the answers from two Cambodian high schools chosen to differ: one in a province and
one in the capital city, so that the data spans real variation in students' answering style and in the
marking teacher. The answers come from semester~1 of the 2026 school year. The original answers are
handwritten examination papers; we digitised each one by hand into machine-readable Khmer text,
together with the question, the reference answer, the teacher's awarded score, and the question's
maximum score. A single experienced high-school subject teacher graded every answer.

\subsection{The Corpus}
The corpus has \textbf{1{,}184} short answers (after dropping one incomplete row) covering \textbf{41}
distinct questions across four subjects (Biology, History, Geography, Earth Science), written by
\textbf{203} students. Each record holds the question, a reference answer, the student's answer, the
teacher's integer score, and the question's maximum score, which varies across
$\{5,6,7,8,10,12,15,20\}$. Table~\ref{tab:descriptive} summarises the corpus, and
Figures~\ref{fig:data-a} and~\ref{fig:data-b} show its main distributions.

To place all questions on a common scale, we give each answer a \emph{normalised score}
\begin{equation}
y \;=\; \frac{\text{Student Score}}{\text{Max Score}} \;\in\; [0,1],
\end{equation}
and an ordinal five-class \emph{grade label}
\begin{equation}
\ell \;=\; \mathrm{clip}\big(\mathrm{round}(4y),\,0,\,4\big)\;\in\;\{0,1,2,3,4\},
\end{equation}
where $y$ is the normalised score and the factor $4$ maps $[0,1]$ onto the five integer grades. As
Table~\ref{tab:descriptive} shows, the labels are heavily skewed toward full credit. Earth Science
contributes only nine answers; it is a minor subject with little data, kept for coverage but
acknowledged as imbalanced.

The four panels in Figures~\ref{fig:data-a} and~\ref{fig:data-b} make the corpus's shape concrete, and
each one carries a fact that matters later. The subject panel (Figure~\ref{fig:data-a}, left) shows that

\subsection{A Real Case of Label Noise: Class 10C Biology}
\label{sec:noisy}
The \texttt{no10c} variant is not an arbitrary deletion; it removes a subset with demonstrable grader
inconsistency. Looking at class~10C, Biology, Question~2 (maximum score~8), the 55 student records
reduce to only \textbf{seven distinct answer texts}, yet \emph{identical} answer texts received scores
spanning almost the whole range (Table~\ref{tab:noise10c}). One and the same answer text was scored 3,
4, 5, 6, 7, and 8. When the same input maps to six different labels, no model can learn a consistent
rule from it, which is why removing this subset lifts every model (\S\ref{sec:results-data}).

\subsection{Data Governance}
The corpus contains real student work by minors. We obtained access by approaching each school and
getting permission directly from the school Director; no formal IRB process applies at this level in
this setting. We store only pseudonymous identifiers (no names), and use the data solely for research
into a teacher-assist tool, not an autonomous grader. Because one teacher graded all answers, the
system reflects that grader's judgements; we report agreement \emph{with this grader} and use the
two dataset variants as partial mitigation.

\section{Data Preprocessing}
\label{sec:preprocessing}
Khmer is written without spaces between words, and the same word can be encoded as different byte
sequences (for example stacked versus unstacked syllables), so the text needs careful cleaning before a
model sees it. We apply the steps in Figure~\ref{fig:preproc}, in order: NFC normalisation; removal of
\emph{invisible} characters (zero-width spaces, control and format characters, and bullet markers such
as $\bullet$ and $\circ$); punctuation stripping; and finally word segmentation with \texttt{khmer-nltk}.
We keep digits and letters as answer content.

Khmer can write the same word in stacked or unstacked form, giving one word several byte sequences; we
rely on the segmenter to identify word boundaries and do not add a separate character-normalisation
step. This keeps the preprocessed text human-readable, so every heatmap and every piece of feedback
shown to a teacher uses these readable tokens directly.

\subsection{Preprocessing Modes and Input Formats}
\label{sec:axes}
Rather than fix one preprocessing recipe, we expose it as an experimental axis with three cumulative
\emph{modes}, so we can measure how much cleaning each model actually needs (Table~\ref{tab:axes}):
\texttt{raw} strips invisible characters and whitespace only; \texttt{clean} adds
punctuation stripping; \texttt{segment} adds \texttt{khmer-nltk} word segmentation on top of
\texttt{clean}. Each mode is a strict superset of the previous one, so a model that improves from
\texttt{raw} to \texttt{segment} is benefiting from the extra Khmer-aware processing.

We also vary how the question, reference, and answer are assembled into the model input, through two
\emph{input formats}: \texttt{ra} feeds the pair (answer, reference), and \texttt{qar} prepends the
question, feeding (question + answer, reference). The \texttt{qar} format gives the model the question
context at the cost of a longer input. Every modelling experiment sweeps these two axes (three
preprocessing modes $\times$ two input formats) together with its model choices, so that each reported
champion is the configuration selected on the validation set rather than a single hand-picked recipe.

\section{Models Used}
\label{sec:pillars}
All models consume the same answer/reference text pair (optionally prefixed with the question) and
output $\hat{y}\in[0,1]$, trained to minimise mean-squared error against the normalised target $y$.

\subsection{Classical: TF-IDF with RBF-SVR}
We represent each text with character-level TF-IDF features (\texttt{char\_wb} $n$-grams of length
2--4, capped at 15{,}000 features), which suit Khmer because they do not depend on perfect
segmentation. For an answer/reference pair with feature vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$, we form
the interaction vector
\begin{equation}
\big[\,\mathbf{a};\ \mathbf{b};\ |\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b}|;\ \mathbf{a}\odot\mathbf{b};\
\cos(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b})\,\big],
\end{equation}
where $;$ is concatenation, $|\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b}|$ the element-wise absolute difference, $\odot$ the
element-wise product, and $\cos$ the cosine similarity. We feed this vector to a support-vector
regressor with an RBF kernel and penalty $C=1$. This model trains in seconds on a CPU.

Within the classical family we also run two simpler reference points: a parameter-free
\texttt{tfidf\_cos} scorer that predicts the cosine similarity between the answer and reference TF-IDF
vectors directly (no training), and a \texttt{fasttext\_cos} scorer that does the same over
100-dimensional fastText embeddings. These bracket the support-vector regressor from below and confirm
that the learned regression, not the TF-IDF representation alone, is what carries the classical result;
the RBF-SVR is the classical champion reported throughout.

\subsection{RNN: Character BiLSTM with Attention}
We embed each text at the character level and encode it with a bidirectional LSTM. An additive
attention layer pools the per-position hidden states $\mathbf{h}_t$ into one vector:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_t=\mathrm{softmax}\!\big(\mathbf{w}^\top \tanh(W\mathbf{h}_t)\big),\qquad
\mathbf{e}=\sum_t \alpha_t \mathbf{h}_t,
\end{equation}
where $W$ and $\mathbf{w}$ are learned, $\alpha_t$ is the weight for position $t$, and $\mathbf{e}$ is
the pooled encoding. We encode the answer and reference separately and combine them through the same
four-way interaction before a small MLP head with a sigmoid output. Concretely, we cap the character
vocabulary at the 5{,}000 most frequent characters, use 128-dimensional character embeddings, two
bidirectional LSTM layers with 128 hidden units per direction, and dropout 0.3, and train with the Adam
optimiser at learning rate $1\mathrm{e}{-}3$, batch size 64, for up to 20 epochs with early stopping
(patience 4) on validation QWK. The attention is part of the architecture; we do \emph{not} use it as an
explanation (we use SHAP, \S\ref{sec:xai}).

\subsection{Transformer: Multilingual Encoders}
We fine-tune multilingual encoders, mBERT, XLM-R, and GTE-multilingual~\citep{devlin2019bert}, in two
configurations: a \emph{dual} encoder that embeds the answer and reference separately and then combines
them with the four-way interaction above, and a \emph{cross} encoder that feeds both texts into one
sequence so that self-attention can compare them token by token. We freeze the bottom six Transformer
layers (the lower layers carry generic, language-agnostic features and freezing them guards against
over-fitting on a small corpus), truncate inputs to 256 tokens, and fine-tune the remaining layers and
a regression head with the AdamW optimiser at learning rate $2\mathrm{e}{-}5$, weight decay 0.01,
dropout 0.2, and batch size 16, for up to 20 epochs with early stopping (patience 4) on validation QWK.
GTE-multilingual ships an unstable rotary position cache that yields NaN outputs; we rebuild the cosine
cache in full (fp32) precision on load to fix this.

\subsection{LLM: Qwen 3.5 4B with QLoRA}
We fine-tune the Qwen 3.5 4B large language model with QLoRA~\citep{dettmers2023qlora}, which trains a
small set of low-rank adapter weights on top of a frozen, 4-bit-quantised (NF4, double-quantised) base
model, making billion-parameter fine-tuning feasible on one GPU. The adapters use rank 16, $\alpha=16$,
no adapter dropout, and target the attention and MLP projections
($q,k,v,o,\mathrm{gate},\mathrm{up},\mathrm{down}$). We frame grading as instruction-following with a
fixed prompt that states the scale, the question, the reference, and the student answer, and asks for a
single integer; the model is trained with completion-only loss (the prompt tokens are masked, so the
loss falls only on the score token):

\begin{quote}\small\ttfamily
Below is a Khmer short-answer grading task. Score the student's answer on a scale from 0 to \{Max\}.\\[2pt]
Question: \{question\}\\
Reference answer: \{reference\}\\
Student answer: \{answer\}\\
The score (integer from 0 to \{Max\}): \{score\}
\end{quote}

\noindent The prompt above is the \texttt{qar} format; in the \texttt{ra} format the \emph{Question}
line is dropped, so the model sees only the student answer and the reference. For instruction-tuned
bases we render this through the model's chat template with reasoning
disabled, so the model emits a bare integer rather than a chain of thought. At inference we greedily
decode, parse the first integer from the output, and clip it to $[0,\text{Max}]$. We fine-tune each base
on the \texttt{no10c} variant in both input formats, for up to
10 epochs at batch size 4 with gradient accumulation to an effective batch of 16, a 5\,\% warm-up, and a
validation-selected learning rate in $\{1,2,3\}\times10^{-4}$, keeping the epoch with the best
validation QWK. The LLM pillar therefore spans two conditions: the \emph{untuned base} prompted
zero-shot (the in-pillar baseline) and its \emph{QLoRA-fine-tuned} counterpart, the KhmerGrader
family. Comparing the two isolates the value of fine-tuning, following the fine-tuning-for-scoring
approach of Latif and Zhai~\citep{latif2024finetuning}; the comparison is reported in
Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

\subsection{Frontier-Model Baselines}
\label{sec:frontier}
To test whether a small fine-tuned specialist can match much larger general models on Khmer, we add a
\emph{frontier baseline}: four proprietary or open frontier LLMs (a GPT, a Claude, a Gemini, and a
DeepSeek model) prompted on the same test answers with the \emph{same} grading prompt as the
KhmerGrader. Each is run \emph{zero-shot} in two variants: a direct integer answer and a
reasoning-enabled answer whose final integer is parsed, so the frontier models are also given their
strongest setting. The score is parsed and clipped exactly as for our own LLM. We grant the frontier
models the reasoning variant precisely because we cannot fine-tune them; our own models are scored
without reasoning, since the fine-tuned KhmerGrader emits a bare integer by design and, of the three
open bases, only Qwen exposes a reasoning mode (the Gemma and the Gemma-based SEA-LION bases do not).
This is also consistent with prior work, where chain-of-thought has not reliably improved ordinal
scoring and adds latency. Few-shot prompting of all models is left to future work (\S\ref{sec:future}).

Using closed APIs as a baseline is a deliberate, scoped choice: it is a one-off \emph{evaluation}
measurement, not a runtime dependency of the prototype, whose feedback always uses an open model. Closed
services are continuously updated, so we \textbf{pin each model version and record the evaluation date},
release the exact prompt and parser, and \textbf{cache every raw API response}; the cached snapshot makes
these numbers reproducible even if a provider later revises the model. We state plainly that closed-model
scores are a dated snapshot, whereas the KhmerGrader weights, code, and data remain fully reproducible.

\subsection{From Score to Grade}
We map the continuous prediction to an integer grade by rounding $4\hat{y}$ (for the five-class label)
or $\hat{y}\times\text{Max Score}$ (for the raw integer score). For example, a prediction
$\hat{y}=0.72$ on a question worth 10 points becomes raw score $\mathrm{round}(0.72\times10)=7$ and grade
label $\mathrm{round}(4\times0.72)=3$. We study two cheap levers. First, because ``0.5'' means 5/10 on
one question but 10/20 on another, we give the model the question's maximum score as an extra input
feature, normalised by the largest observed maximum (20) so it lands in $(0,1]$; this gives consistent
deployment gains. Second, \emph{post-hoc threshold calibration} fits the four cut-points that map
$\hat{y}$ to the five classes by maximising QWK on the validation set. We treat calibration as an
ablation, not part of the headline, because (as Chapter~\ref{ch:results} shows) it helps the classical
model but hurts the BiLSTM, so all headline numbers are uncalibrated.

\section{Model Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

\subsection{Agreement and Deployment Metrics}
Our primary metric is \emph{Quadratic Weighted Kappa} (QWK), the field standard for ordinal scoring.
For confusion matrix $O$, expected matrix $E$, and quadratic weights $w_{ij}=(i-j)^2/(K-1)^2$ for $K$
classes,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{QWK} \;=\; 1 - \frac{\sum_{i,j} w_{ij}\,O_{ij}}{\sum_{i,j} w_{ij}\,E_{ij}},
\end{equation}
so QWK${}=1$ is perfect agreement, $0$ is chance, and large disagreements are penalised much more than
small ones. For the comparison with the Arabic anchor~\citep{alaoui2024} we also report unweighted
Cohen's $\kappa$. We report \emph{accuracy} and \emph{macro-F1}:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Acc}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\mathbf{1}[\hat{\ell}_n=\ell_n],\qquad
\mathrm{macro\text{-}F1}=\frac{1}{K}\sum_{c=1}^{K}\frac{2P_cR_c}{P_c+R_c},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{1}[\cdot]$ is the indicator, and $P_c$, $R_c$ are the precision and recall of class $c$;
macro-averaging weights every grade equally, which matters under the heavy class imbalance. For
deployment we report \emph{exact agreement} (predicted integer score equals the teacher's) and
\emph{adjacent agreement} (within one point):
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{exact}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_n \mathbf{1}[\hat{r}_n=r_n],\qquad
\mathrm{adj}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_n \mathbf{1}[\,|\hat{r}_n-r_n|\le 1\,],
\end{equation}
where $r_n$ and $\hat{r}_n$ are the true and predicted raw integer scores in points.

\subsection{Explainability Protocol}
\label{sec:xai}
Our explanation is \emph{word attribution}: we highlight which words in the student's answer drove the
predicted grade. Following the explainable-scoring approach of Kumar and
Boulanger~\citep{kumar2020explainable}, the engine is \emph{SHAP} (SHapley Additive
exPlanations)~\citep{lundberg2017shap}. The attribution of a word is its Shapley value, the average
marginal change in the predicted score as that word is added to the answer over all coalitions of the
other words,
\begin{equation}
\phi_i \;=\; \sum_{S \subseteq W\setminus\{i\}} \frac{|S|!\,(|W|-|S|-1)!}{|W|!}\,
\big[\,s(S\cup\{i\}) - s(S)\,\big],
\end{equation}
where $W$ is the set of answer words, $S$ a coalition (a subset of the kept words), and $s(\cdot)$ the
score predicted on the answer restricted to those words; a positive $\phi_i$ means the word raised the
grade. We estimate the Shapley values with a permutation explainer (with a Monte-Carlo permutation
fallback), holding the reference side fixed. SHAP is the field-standard attribution and is
\emph{model-agnostic}: it calls only $s(\cdot)$, so it works for every pillar including the
non-differentiable classical SVR, with no gradients or model internals. Beyond the per-answer
attribution, we rank the \emph{globally} most-important words by their mean absolute SHAP value across
answers, mirroring Kumar's global-importance view.

We judge an explanation by its \emph{plausibility}~\citep{lyu2024faithfulsurvey}: whether the words it
highlights look right to a teacher. A teacher grades a short answer by checking whether it carries the
key content of the reference answer, so a plausible explanation should highlight answer words that also
appear in the reference. We therefore measure plausibility as the fraction of the top-$k$ SHAP words
($k=20\,\%$ of the answer words) that occur in the reference answer; it lies in $[0,1]$ and is
computable for every pillar.
