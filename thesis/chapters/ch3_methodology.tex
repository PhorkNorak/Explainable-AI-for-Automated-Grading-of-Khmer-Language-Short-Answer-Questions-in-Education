\section{Research Design}
\label{sec:design}
We use a quantitative, experiment-driven design. We frame all four model families as the same task,
\emph{bounded ordinal regression}, and train and evaluate them through one shared, Khmer-aware
pipeline, so that differences in results come from the models rather than from inconsistent
preprocessing or evaluation. Each model predicts a continuous normalised score $\hat{y}\in[0,1]$, which
we map to an integer grade for reporting. We then judge every model on two axes: standard agreement
metrics \emph{and} the faithfulness of its explanations. Figure~\ref{fig:method} shows the overall
research pipeline.

\section{Data Collection}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Provenance}
We built the corpus from \emph{real} semester examination answers, not synthetic or crowd-sourced
text. We collected the answers from two Cambodian high schools chosen to differ: one in a province and
one in the capital city, so that the data spans real variation in students' answering style and in the
marking teacher. The answers come from semester~1 of the 2026 school year. The original answers are
handwritten examination papers; we digitised each one by hand into machine-readable Khmer text,
together with the question, the reference answer, the teacher's awarded score, and the question's
maximum score. A single experienced high-school subject teacher graded every answer.

\subsection{The Corpus}
The corpus has \textbf{1{,}184} short answers (after dropping one incomplete row) covering \textbf{41}
distinct questions across four subjects (Biology, History, Geography, Earth Science), written by
\textbf{203} students. Each record holds the question, a reference answer, the student's answer, the
teacher's integer score, and the question's maximum score, which varies across
$\{5,6,7,8,10,12,15,20\}$. Table~\ref{tab:descriptive} summarises the corpus, and
Figures~\ref{fig:data-a} and~\ref{fig:data-b} show its main distributions.

To place all questions on a common scale, we give each answer a \emph{normalised score}
\begin{equation}
y \;=\; \frac{\text{Student Score}}{\text{Max Score}} \;\in\; [0,1],
\end{equation}
and an ordinal five-class \emph{grade label}
\begin{equation}
\ell \;=\; \mathrm{clip}\big(\mathrm{round}(4y),\,0,\,4\big)\;\in\;\{0,1,2,3,4\},
\end{equation}
where $y$ is the normalised score and the factor $4$ maps $[0,1]$ onto the five integer grades. As
Table~\ref{tab:descriptive} shows, the labels are heavily skewed toward full credit. Earth Science
contributes only nine answers; it is a minor subject with little data, kept for coverage but
acknowledged as imbalanced.

Unless stated otherwise, we split the data 70/15/15 into training, validation, and test sets with a

no question is shared between train, validation, and test (\S\ref{sec:metrics}).

\subsection{A Real Case of Label Noise: Class 10C Biology}
\label{sec:noisy}
The \texttt{no10c} variant is not an arbitrary deletion; it removes a subset with demonstrable grader
inconsistency. Looking at class~10C, Biology, Question~1 (maximum score~8), the 55 student records
reduce to only \textbf{seven distinct answer texts}, yet \emph{identical} answer texts received scores
spanning almost the whole range (Table~\ref{tab:noise10c}). One and the same answer text was scored 3,
4, 5, 6, 7, and 8. When the same input maps to six different labels, no model can learn a consistent
rule from it, which is why removing this subset lifts every model (\S\ref{sec:results-data}).

\subsection{Data Governance}
The corpus contains real student work by minors. We obtained access by approaching each school and
getting permission directly from the school Director; no formal IRB process applies at this level in
this setting. We store only pseudonymous identifiers (no names), and use the data solely for research
into a teacher-assist tool, not an autonomous grader. Because one teacher graded all answers, the
system reflects that grader's judgements; we report agreement \emph{with this grader} and use the
three dataset variants as partial mitigation.

\section{Data Preprocessing}
\label{sec:preprocessing}
Khmer text needs careful normalisation because the script encodes a syllable as an ordered cluster of
a base consonant, optional subscript consonants, vowels, and signs, and the same visible syllable can
be stored as different byte sequences. We apply the steps in Figure~\ref{fig:preproc}, in order: NFC
normalisation; removal of \emph{invisible} characters (zero-width spaces, control and format
characters, and bullet markers such as $\bullet$ and $\circ$); \emph{KCC} (Khmer Character Cluster) reordering,
which canonicalises the internal order of base consonants, subscripts (\km{ជើង}), vowels, and signs;
punctuation stripping; and finally word segmentation with \texttt{khmer-nltk}. We keep digits and
letters as answer content.

KCC reordering is essential for the model but produces text that is \emph{unreadable} to a human. For
example, a syllable a person reads as \km{ស៊ី} may be reordered internally to a form that renders as
\km{សី៊}: identical for the model but visibly wrong to a teacher. We therefore separate
\emph{model-internal} text (with KCC reorder) from \emph{display} text (the readable original tokens,
with no KCC reorder). Every heatmap and every piece of feedback shown to a teacher uses the readable
display tokens.

\section{Models Used}
\label{sec:pillars}
All models consume the same answer/reference text pair (optionally prefixed with the question) and
output $\hat{y}\in[0,1]$, trained to minimise mean-squared error against the normalised target $y$.

\subsection{Classical: TF-IDF with RBF-SVR}
We represent each text with character-level TF-IDF features (\texttt{char\_wb} $n$-grams of length
2--4, capped at 15{,}000 features), which suit Khmer because they do not depend on perfect
segmentation. For an answer/reference pair with feature vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$, we form
the interaction vector
\begin{equation}
\big[\,\mathbf{a};\ \mathbf{b};\ |\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b}|;\ \mathbf{a}\odot\mathbf{b};\
\cos(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b})\,\big],
\end{equation}
where $;$ is concatenation, $|\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b}|$ the element-wise absolute difference, $\odot$ the
element-wise product, and $\cos$ the cosine similarity. We feed this vector to a support-vector
regressor with an RBF kernel. This model trains in seconds on a CPU.

\subsection{RNN: Character BiLSTM with Attention}
We embed each text at the character level and encode it with a bidirectional LSTM. An additive
attention layer pools the per-position hidden states $\mathbf{h}_t$ into one vector:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_t=\mathrm{softmax}\!\big(\mathbf{w}^\top \tanh(W\mathbf{h}_t)\big),\qquad
\mathbf{e}=\sum_t \alpha_t \mathbf{h}_t,
\end{equation}
where $W$ and $\mathbf{w}$ are learned, $\alpha_t$ is the weight for position $t$, and $\mathbf{e}$ is
the pooled encoding. We encode the answer and reference separately and combine them through the same
four-way interaction before a small MLP head with a sigmoid output. The attention is part of the
architecture; we do \emph{not} use it as an explanation (we use LOO, \S\ref{sec:xai}).

\subsection{Transformer: Multilingual Encoders}
We fine-tune multilingual encoders, mBERT, XLM-R, and GTE-multilingual~\citep{devlin2019bert}, in two
configurations: a \emph{dual} encoder that embeds answer and reference separately, and a \emph{cross}
encoder that feeds both into one sequence. We freeze the bottom six layers and truncate inputs to 256
tokens. (GTE-multilingual ships an unstable rotary position cache; we rebuild it in full precision on
load to avoid NaN outputs.)

\subsection{LLM: Qwen 3.5 4B with QLoRA}
We fine-tune the Qwen 3.5 4B large language model with QLoRA~\citep{dettmers2023qlora}, which trains a
small set of low-rank adapter weights on top of a frozen, 4-bit-quantised base model, making
billion-parameter fine-tuning feasible on one GPU. We prompt the model with the question, reference,
and student answer, and train it to emit the score. The fine-tuned checkpoints form the KhmerGrader
family, each compared against its own zero-shot base (Chapter~\ref{ch:results}).

\subsection{From Score to Grade}
We map the continuous prediction to an integer grade by rounding $4\hat{y}$ (for the five-class label)
or $\hat{y}\times\text{Max Score}$ (for the raw integer score). We study two cheap levers (RQ4). First,
because ``0.5'' means 5/10 on one question but 10/20 on another, we give the model the question's
maximum score as an extra input feature; this gives consistent deployment gains. Second, \emph{post-hoc
threshold calibration} fits the four cut-points that map $\hat{y}$ to the five classes by maximising QWK
on the validation set. We treat calibration as an ablation, not part of the headline, because (as
Chapter~\ref{ch:results} shows) it helps the classical model but hurts the BiLSTM, so all headline
numbers are uncalibrated.

\section{Model Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

\subsection{Agreement and Deployment Metrics}
Our primary metric is \emph{Quadratic Weighted Kappa} (QWK), the field standard for ordinal scoring.
For confusion matrix $O$, expected matrix $E$, and quadratic weights $w_{ij}=(i-j)^2/(K-1)^2$ for $K$
classes,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{QWK} \;=\; 1 - \frac{\sum_{i,j} w_{ij}\,O_{ij}}{\sum_{i,j} w_{ij}\,E_{ij}},
\end{equation}
so QWK${}=1$ is perfect agreement, $0$ is chance, and large disagreements are penalised much more than
small ones. For the comparison with the Arabic anchor~\citep{alaoui2024} we also report unweighted
Cohen's $\kappa$. We report \emph{accuracy} and \emph{macro-F1}:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Acc}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\mathbf{1}[\hat{\ell}_n=\ell_n],\qquad
\mathrm{macro\text{-}F1}=\frac{1}{K}\sum_{c=1}^{K}\frac{2P_cR_c}{P_c+R_c},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{1}[\cdot]$ is the indicator, and $P_c$, $R_c$ are the precision and recall of class $c$;
macro-averaging weights every grade equally, which matters under the heavy class imbalance. For
deployment we report \emph{exact agreement} (predicted integer score equals the teacher's) and
\emph{adjacent agreement} (within one point):
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{exact}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_n \mathbf{1}[\hat{r}_n=r_n],\qquad
\mathrm{adj}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_n \mathbf{1}[\,|\hat{r}_n-r_n|\le 1\,],
\end{equation}
where $r_n$ and $\hat{r}_n$ are the true and predicted raw integer scores in points.

\subsection{Explainability Protocol}
\label{sec:xai}
Our explanation is \emph{word attribution}: we highlight which words in the student's answer drove the
predicted grade. The engine is \emph{occlusion} (leave-one-out, LOO), an attribution shown to be
reliable for ASAG~\citep{pinto2025attribution}. As Figure~\ref{fig:loo} shows, we drop one word and
measure the change in score; the importance of word $i$ is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{imp}(i) \;=\; s(\text{answer}) \;-\; s(\text{answer without word } i),
\end{equation}
where $s(\cdot)$ is the predicted score, so a positive value means the word supported the grade. We use
LOO as the single method because it works for every model, including the non-differentiable classical
SVR, needs no gradients or model internals, and is deterministic.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[font=\footnotesize, >={Latex[length=2mm]}, node distance=5mm,
  b/.style={draw, rounded corners, fill=blue!6, align=center, inner sep=4pt, text width=3.3cm,

  \node[b, below=8mm of full] (drop) {answer without word $i$ \\ score $s(\text{answer}\setminus i)$};
  \node[b, right=28mm of full, yshift=-7mm, fill=green!8, text width=3.7cm] (imp)

  \draw[->,thick] (full.east) -- (imp.west);
  \draw[->,thick] (drop.east) -- (imp.west);
\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{How LOO word attribution works: drop a word, the change in score is its importance.}
\label{fig:loo}
\end{figure}

We judge explanations on two axes~\citep{lyu2024faithfulsurvey}. \emph{Faithfulness} asks whether the
explanation reflects the model's reasoning, measured by the ERASER metrics~\citep{deyoung2020eraser}.
Let $s(x)$ be the score on the full answer, $x_{\setminus k}$ the answer with the top-$k\%$ most
important words removed, and $x_k$ the answer keeping only those words; then
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{comp}_k = s(x) - s(x_{\setminus k}),\qquad \mathrm{suff}_k = s(x) - s(x_{k}),
\end{equation}
where \emph{comprehensiveness} should be high (removing important words drops the score) and
\emph{sufficiency} should be low (the top words alone reproduce the score). We average each over
$k\in\{10,20,30,40,50\}\%$ as the AOPC summary, and compare against a random-removal baseline; the
\emph{faithfulness gap} (comprehensiveness minus its random baseline) is positive when the explanation
beats random. \emph{Plausibility} is the fraction of top important words that also appear in the
reference answer.

\subsection{Question-Held-Out Evaluation}
To measure question leakage (RQ6), we re-evaluate the classical champion under the question-held-out
split (no question shared between train/validation/test), across five seeds. With only 41 questions,
each unseen-question test set has about six questions, so this estimate is high-variance, which we
report as the spread across seeds.
