\chapter{Results and Discussion}
\label{ch:results}

\textbf{How to read the numbers.} Two numbers matter most: \textbf{QWK} (how well a model's grades
agree with the teacher overall) and \textbf{exact match} (how often it gives the same mark out of $N$).
All headline values are \emph{uncalibrated}. The classical, BiLSTM, and LLM champions share the 909
(\texttt{no10c}) test set; the encoder was selected on the \texttt{full} variant, so cross-pillar QWK
is indicative, not a strict head-to-head.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Per-Pillar Accuracy (RQ1)}
\label{sec:results-pillars}

exact mark. Table~\ref{tab:champs} gives the numbers, and Figures~\ref{fig:metrics}--\ref{fig:predvtrue}
show them.

Reading Table~\ref{tab:champs} model by model, ranked by QWK:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item The \textbf{RNN (BiLSTM)} reaches the highest QWK at \textbf{0.845}, agreeing with the teacher
  most closely overall, though its exact-mark rate (0.54) trails the LLM.
  \item The \textbf{LLM (Qwen)} follows at QWK \textbf{0.843}, effectively level with the RNN, yet it
  clearly leads every deployment metric: accuracy 0.80, macro-F1 0.78, exact 0.66, and adjacent 0.83.
  \item The \textbf{Transformer (GTE)} scores QWK \textbf{0.820} with the second-best exact mark
  (0.57), close behind on overall agreement.
  \item The \textbf{Classical (SVR)} sits at QWK \textbf{0.795}, within 0.05 of the best model despite
  training in about 30 seconds on a CPU, but its exact mark (0.20) and macro-F1 (0.42) are the weakest.
\end{itemize}
These results show the four pillars sit in a narrow 0.05 QWK band, so there is no meaningful research
``winner''; the only clear separation is on the deployment metrics, where the fine-tuned LLM
leads. The comparable-band claim concerns the four \emph{trained} pillars: the same Qwen base scores
only \textbf{0.500} QWK zero-shot (Table~\ref{tab:champs}, last row), so the LLM pillar's standing is
earned entirely by QLoRA fine-tuning (a $+0.34$ lift). Figure~\ref{fig:qwk} makes the band visible: the vertical axis is test QWK and each bar is one
pillar, with the two dotted lines marking the lowest and highest score. The gap between those lines is
only 0.050 QWK, so the four bars are nearly level; this is the visual form of the thesis's first
finding, that on the research metric the expensive models do not pull ahead of the cheap ones.

\noindent\textbf{Key insight (RQ1).} On the primary research metric the four trained pillars are
comparable (a 0.05 QWK band), so a 30-second CPU model agrees with the teacher about as well as a
fine-tuned LLM; the LLM's value, shown next, is in deployment, and the same base needs fine-tuning to
reach the band at all.

Two cross-cutting points hold across the table. First, macro-F1 lags accuracy for every model except
the LLM (classical 0.42 versus 0.63), because the data is skewed to full credit and the cheaper models
handle the rare grades poorly. Figure~\ref{fig:prf} shows this class-balance story directly: for each
pillar it plots macro-averaged precision, recall, and F1 side by side, where ``macro'' means every grade
counts equally regardless of how rare it is. The classical, RNN, and Transformer pillars all sit near
0.4 to 0.55 on all three measures, while the LLM jumps to about 0.78 on each, confirming that the LLM is
the only model that handles the rare partial-credit grades rather than defaulting to the common ones.
Second, there is no hidden over-fitting (Figure~\ref{fig:metrics}): the
classical model has the widest train-to-test gap (accuracy 0.79 versus 0.63), while the deep pillars
stay tight (the LLM 0.83 versus 0.80). Training QWK does reach 0.90+ (up to 0.91 for the classical and
RNN pillars), but rather than tune for the highest training fit, we select every model by validation QWK
with early stopping and report the honest test numbers, deliberately favouring a small train-to-test gap
over a higher but over-fitted training score.

\subsection{Research versus Deployment (RQ2)}
\label{sec:results-deploy}
The model that is best for research (QWK) is not what wins in the classroom. Because the four pillars
are comparable on QWK, the decision lies elsewhere. On the metrics a teacher cares about, the LLM wins:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{Exact mark:} LLM 0.66, ahead of the encoder (0.57) and the classical model (0.20).
  \item \textbf{Within one point:} LLM 0.83.
  \item \textbf{Lowest average point error} (MAE $\approx$ 0.93 points).
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:deploy} shows the deployment gap. For each pillar it places two bars, the fraction of
test answers scored \emph{exactly} right and the fraction scored within one point, both on the same
0-to-1 vertical axis. The within-one-point bars are high for every model, but the exact-match bars fan
out sharply: the LLM's reaches 0.66 while the classical model's sits at 0.20. A teacher reading these
bars sees how often each tool would hand back a mark they could accept unchanged.

Figure~\ref{fig:scoredist} explains \emph{why} the LLM wins here. It overlays two histograms on the
points axis, the human marks and the LLM's predicted marks, on the same test answers. The two
distributions have the same shape, including the spike at full credit, rather than the predicted one
bunching up in the middle. The cheaper regression models, by minimising squared error, tend to hedge
toward the common middle grades; the LLM instead reproduces the full spread of marks a teacher actually
awards. The practical guidance follows: pick the tool by the goal, the cheap self-explaining classical
model for a quick check, the fine-tuned LLM when the exact mark matters.

\noindent\textbf{Key insight (RQ2).} Research agreement and deployment quality come apart: the pillars
tie on QWK, but only the fine-tuned LLM reproduces the teacher's \emph{exact} mark often enough to hand
back unchanged, so the two questions must be reported separately.

\subsection{Design-Choice Ablations}
\label{sec:results-data}
Each axis of the grid is studied one factor at a time, holding the others fixed; the full per-cell
leaderboards for every combination are in Appendix~\ref{app:grid}.

\paragraph{Data variant.} Cleaning the data helped as much as changing the model. Holding the model
fixed (GTE dual encoder with the max-score feature), QWK rises $0.820 \to 0.842$ from \texttt{full} to
\texttt{no10c}, a $+0.022$ gain from \emph{removing the inconsistently graded 10C subset alone}
(\S\ref{sec:noisy}, Figure~\ref{fig:cleaning}), larger than the gaps between architectures. A separate
cleaning refinement (stripping zero-width/format noise) changes the classical QWK by at most 0.004, so
the gain comes from \emph{which data} is kept, not cosmetic text processing.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{fig_cleaning_ablation.png}
\caption[Effect of cleaning refinement on classical QWK]{Classical QWK under the older versus refined
cleaning recipe for each dataset variant, with the change labelled. The negligible movement shows
cleaning refinements matter far less than which subset of data is kept.}
\label{fig:cleaning}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Preprocessing mode.} Across the \texttt{raw}/\texttt{clean}/\texttt{segment} ladder, each
family's champion selects the representation that suits it: the classical char-$n$-gram model wins with
\texttt{segment} (word boundaries help a bag-of-$n$-grams), while the BiLSTM, encoder, and LLM prefer
\texttt{clean} and do not need the segmentation crutch.

\paragraph{Input format.} Between \texttt{ra} (answer + reference) and \texttt{qar} (question + answer +
reference), the surface models (classical, BiLSTM, encoder champions) win with \texttt{ra}: the question
text is identical across a question's answers, so it adds no discriminative signal for a lexical model.
The LLM benefits from \texttt{qar} because it can actually read and use the question.

\paragraph{Max-score feature.} Concatenating the question's normalised maximum score gives consistent,
near-free deployment gains (about $+4.5$ points of exact match for the neural heads on the 909 set)
because it tells the model the per-question scale rather than refitting boundaries on a small
validation set.

\paragraph{Calibration.} Post-hoc threshold calibration helps the \emph{classical} model (test QWK
$0.795 \to 0.847$) but \emph{hurts} the BiLSTM (test QWK $0.845 \to 0.818$) under the same
validation-selected rule (Figure~\ref{fig:calib}). The cut-points are fit on only $\sim$134 validation
rows, so the gain does not transfer reliably; calibration is therefore reported as an ablation and the
headline numbers are uncalibrated.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{fig_calibration.png}
\caption[Effect of calibration by pillar]{Uncalibrated versus calibrated test QWK per pillar, with the
change labelled. Calibration helps the classical model but hurts the BiLSTM, so it is fragile and
model-dependent rather than a reliable gain.}
\label{fig:calib}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Hyperparameters.} The classical champion's test QWK moves by less than 0.002 across the SVR
penalty and TF-IDF feature-count sweep (Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments}, Figure~\ref{fig:hparam}), so the
result does not depend on a lucky hyperparameter choice.

\noindent\textbf{Key insight.} On this corpus \emph{which data} is kept moves the needle as much as the
architecture, and the cheap max-score feature is a reliable lever; the remaining factors (preprocessing,
input format, calibration, hyperparameters) are second-order or model-dependent.

\subsection{Explainability (RQ3)}
\label{sec:results-xai}
The word highlights point at the content a teacher looks for, and they do so for every model. The method
is \emph{SHAP} word attribution (\S\ref{sec:xai}), following the explainable-scoring work of Kumar and
Boulanger~\citep{kumar2020explainable}. SHAP is model-agnostic, so the same method explains all four
pillars, including the non-differentiable classical SVR. We report what Kumar reports: a per-answer
\emph{local} explanation (the heatmap), a \emph{global} ranking of the words that matter most across
answers, and a plausibility check (the overlap of the highlighted words with the reference answer).
The classical, BiLSTM, and encoder pillars run a full SHAP sweep; for the LLM, where each SHAP
evaluation is a full generation, we run SHAP under a \emph{capped} evaluation budget so plausibility is
still computed for every pillar, with illustrative LLM heatmaps in Appendix~\ref{app:heatmaps}.

Reading Table~\ref{tab:xai}, the classical model is the most plausible of the computed pillars
(0.66): its character $n$-gram features key on content words that also appear in the reference answer,
so about two of every three highlighted words are rubric-relevant; the BiLSTM is close behind (0.59),
and the encoder and LLM plausibilities are HPC-pending.
The top SHAP words are mostly content words rather than function words, which is what makes the
explanation a usable basis for teacher feedback.

\paragraph{Globally important words.} Aggregating the SHAP values over the explained answers gives a
global ranking of the words a model relies on (Kumar's global-importance view). For the classical model
the highest-ranked words are mostly subject-content terms, for example \km{សេដ្ឋកិច្ច} (economy),
\km{ការនេសាទ} (fishing), \km{ប្រទេស} (country), \km{ប្រជាជន} (people), and \km{កម្ពុជា} (Cambodia),
confirming that the model grades largely on the content of the answer rather than on surface form.

Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} shows the local view: an example SHAP heatmap on a real Khmer answer from the
classical model. The text is the student's answer in readable tokens, and each
word is shaded by its SHAP value, the word's contribution to the predicted grade; darker means a larger
contribution. The reader can see at a glance which words earned the score, which is the point of showing
an explanation rather than a bare number, and is what lets the prototype turn the same attribution into
formative feedback. The other pillars' SHAP heatmaps on real answers appear in
Appendix~\ref{app:heatmaps}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
Taking the models in turn, the results follow from how each one represents a Khmer answer.

The \textbf{Classical} model (TF-IDF with RBF-SVR) stays within 0.05 QWK of the best pillar because
character $n$-gram overlap is a strong signal for short factual answers, and the support-vector
regressor is hard to over-fit on a small corpus. That same lexical nature explains its weak exact mark
(0.20) and macro-F1 (0.42): regression on word overlap smooths predictions toward the common grades and
cannot resolve the fine partial-credit distinctions a teacher makes.

The \textbf{RNN (BiLSTM)} reaches the highest QWK (0.845) because additive attention over a character
BiLSTM captures order and local context that bag-of-$n$-grams misses, while staying small enough to fit
the roughly 600 training rows without over-fitting. It still trails the LLM on the exact mark, as it
predicts a single normalised score rather than reasoning about the rubric.

The \textbf{Transformer (GTE)} lands in the middle (QWK 0.820) because the multilingual encoder brings
useful contextual representations but only thin Khmer pre-training, so it does not clearly beat the much
cheaper pillars on this corpus, even though its exact mark (0.57) is strong.

The \textbf{LLM (Qwen)} wins every deployment metric (exact 0.66, adjacent 0.83) because QLoRA
fine-tuning lets a generative model learn the scoring rubric directly from the question, reference, and
answer, producing marks that follow the human distribution instead of collapsing toward the mean. Its
QWK (0.843) is only level with the others, so its advantage is in deployment accuracy, not overall
agreement.

Putting the three axes together, the ``best model'' question is genuinely three-dimensional: the four
pillars are comparable on research agreement (QWK), the fine-tuned LLM wins deployment (the exact mark)
clearly, and SHAP gives a plausible word-level explanation for every model.

We can also place the numbers against the Arabic anchor~\citep{alaoui2024} in two careful ways. First,
on the \emph{same} unweighted Cohen's $\kappa$, our models score 0.47 (classical), about 0.62 (encoder
and RNN), and 0.71 (LLM), versus their 0.48 (BERT) and 0.60 (best transformer); this is context, not a
leaderboard, because the datasets differ. Second, and more fairly, the train/test \emph{generalisation
gap}: their best transformer over-fits by $+18.5$ points (95.67/77.22 accuracy), whereas our deep cells
are far tighter ($+1.4$ for the GTE encoder; $+3.0$ for Qwen, 83.3/80.3), and even the classical model
($+15.6$, 78.6/63.0) stays under their transformer. There is no hidden over-fitting beneath our
numbers.

For a classroom, the implication is concrete: a cheap classical model that explains itself with SHAP
is an attractive default; the LLM is the right choice when the exact mark matters; and in both cases
the explanation shown to a teacher should highlight words that are plausible, matching the reference content. The
