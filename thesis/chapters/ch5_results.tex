\chapter{Results and Discussion}
\label{ch:results}

\textbf{How to read the numbers.} Two numbers matter most: \textbf{QWK} (how well a model's grades
agree with the teacher overall) and \textbf{exact match} (how often it gives the same mark out of $N$).
All headline values are \emph{uncalibrated}. The classical and BiLSTM champions share the 895 test set;
the encoder and LLM were selected on different variants, so cross-pillar QWK is indicative, not a
strict head-to-head.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Per-Pillar Performance}
\label{sec:results-pillars}
The four models agree with the teacher about equally well overall, but the LLM is clearly best at the
exact mark. Table~\ref{tab:champs} gives the numbers, and Figures~\ref{fig:metrics}--\ref{fig:predvtrue}
show them.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Show the per-pillar champion results (uncalibrated; random split; macro-averaged F1).}
\label{tab:champs}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccccc@{}}
\toprule
Pillar & QWK & Accuracy & macro-F1 & Exact & Adjacent ($\pm1$) \\
\midrule
Classical (SVR)   & 0.795 & 0.63 & 0.42 & 0.20 & 0.71 \\
RNN (BiLSTM)      & \textbf{0.845} & 0.75 & 0.53 & 0.54 & 0.77 \\
Transformer (GTE) & 0.820 & 0.73 & 0.54 & 0.57 & 0.77 \\
LLM (Qwen)        & 0.843 & \textbf{0.80} & \textbf{0.78} & \textbf{0.66} & \textbf{0.83} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

We find that:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{QWK is comparable} across all four pillars (0.795 to 0.845, a narrow 0.05 band;
  Table~\ref{tab:champs}), so there is no meaningful QWK ``winner''.
  \item A \textbf{30-second CPU classical model} sits within 0.05 QWK of a fine-tuned LLM, so on this
  real Khmer corpus the expensive model does \emph{not} win the research metric (RQ1).
  \item The \textbf{LLM clearly wins the rest}: accuracy 0.80, macro-F1 0.78, exact 0.66, adjacent
  0.83 (Table~\ref{tab:champs}).
  \item \textbf{macro-F1 lags accuracy} for every model except the LLM (classical 0.42 vs 0.63),
  because the data is skewed to full credit and the cheaper models handle the rare grades poorly.
  \item \textbf{No hidden over-fitting} (Figure~\ref{fig:metrics}): the classical model has the widest
  train-to-test gap (accuracy 0.79 vs 0.63), while the deep pillars stay tight (the LLM 0.83 vs 0.80).
  \item \textbf{We do not chase the training score.} Training QWK does reach \textbf{0.90+} (up to 0.91
  for the classical and RNN pillars); rather than tune for the highest training fit, we select every
  model by validation QWK with early stopping and report the honest test numbers, deliberately
  favouring a small train-to-test gap over a higher but over-fitted training score.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Research versus Deployment}
\label{sec:results-deploy}
The model that is best for research (QWK) is not what wins in the classroom. Because the four pillars
are comparable on QWK, the decision lies elsewhere. On the metrics a teacher cares about, the LLM wins
(RQ3):

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{Exact mark:} LLM 0.66, ahead of the encoder (0.57) and the classical model (0.20).
  \item \textbf{Within one point:} LLM 0.83.
  \item \textbf{Lowest average point error} (MAE $\approx$ 0.93 points).
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:deploy} shows the deployment gap, and Figure~\ref{fig:scoredist} shows that the LLM's
predicted marks follow the human distribution instead of collapsing toward the mean. The practical
guidance: pick the tool by the goal, the cheap self-explaining classical model for a quick check, the
fine-tuned LLM when the exact mark matters.

\subsection{Data Quality and Calibration}
\label{sec:results-data}
Cleaning the data helped as much as changing the model, and calibration is unreliable (RQ2, RQ4).
Holding the model fixed (GTE dual encoder with the max-score feature), QWK rises
$0.820 \to 0.842 \to 0.847$ across the \texttt{full}, \texttt{no10c}, and \texttt{no10c\_no0} variants,
a $+0.027$ gain from \emph{data cleaning alone} (Figure~\ref{fig:cleaning}), larger than the gaps
between architectures. The biggest single step is removing the inconsistently graded 10C subset
(\S\ref{sec:noisy}); a later cleaning refinement changes the classical QWK by at most 0.004.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{fig_cleaning_ablation.png}
\caption{Show how data cleaning changes the classical QWK on each dataset variant.}
\label{fig:cleaning}
\end{figure}

Post-hoc threshold calibration helps the \emph{classical} model (test QWK $0.795 \to 0.847$) but
\emph{hurts} the BiLSTM (test QWK $0.845 \to 0.818$) using the same validation-selected rule
(Figure~\ref{fig:calib}). The cut-points are fit on only $\sim$134 validation rows, so the gain does
not transfer; this is why calibration is an ablation and the headline numbers are uncalibrated. The
max-score feature, by contrast, gives consistent deployment gains (about $+4.5$ points of exact match
for the neural heads on the 895 set) because it tells the model the per-question scale.

\subsection{The LLM Fine-Tuning Lift}
\label{sec:results-llm}
The LLM pillar is the KhmerGrader family: three open base models fine-tuned identically with QLoRA, to
test whether the base model matters once fine-tuned. We compare each with its own zero-shot base on the
same 909 test set (Table~\ref{tab:llmfamily}, Figure~\ref{fig:llmgain}). This mirrors recent practice
of comparing fine-tuned against prompted LLMs for grading~\citep{lachana2025dutch} and of fine-tuning
open LLMs such as Qwen for scoring~\citep{xu2025qwenscore}.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Show the KhmerGrader family results: zero-shot base vs QLoRA fine-tuned (909 test set).}
\label{tab:llmfamily}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccccc@{}}
\toprule
KhmerGrader model & Base size & Zero-shot QWK & Fine-tuned QWK & Lift & Exact \\
\midrule
Qwen-KhmerGrader-4B       & 4B   & 0.500    & \textbf{0.843} & $+0.34$ & 0.657 \\
Gemma-KhmerGrader-4B      & 4B   & $-0.082$ & 0.763          & $+0.85$ & 0.657 \\
SEA-LION-KhmerGrader-E2B  & 2.3B & 0.541    & 0.802          & $+0.26$ & \textbf{0.693} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

We find that:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{fine-tuning is decisive}: zero-shot Khmer grading is poor (the Gemma base is worse than
  chance at QWK $-0.082$); QLoRA lifts all three bases into the 0.76 to 0.84 band;
  \item \textbf{Qwen is the QWK champion} (0.843), but the much smaller, region-adapted SEA-LION base
  (about 2.3B) gives the \emph{best} exact match (0.693);
  \item \textbf{the worst starting base lifts most} (Gemma $+0.85$), so the fine-tuning data, not the
  base's prior Khmer ability, drives the result;
  \item within the Qwen champion, data cleaning matters again: QWK is 0.743, 0.843, 0.810 on
  \texttt{full}, \texttt{no10c}, \texttt{no10c\_no0}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Explainability}
\label{sec:results-xai}
The word highlights are trustworthy for every model, strongest for the small BiLSTM and weakest (but
still good) for the LLM (RQ5). The method is Leave-One-Out (LOO) word attribution. Unlike most prior
scoring-XAI work, which relies on SHAP~\citep{kumar2020explainable,lachana2025dutch}, LOO is one
model-agnostic method that also works on the non-differentiable classical model, and we \emph{measure}
its faithfulness. Table~\ref{tab:xai} reports faithfulness on 135 test answers.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Show the LOO faithfulness of each model ($n{=}135$); higher comprehensiveness and lower
sufficiency are better. ``Gap'' beats a random baseline when $>0$; ``Plaus.'' is reference overlap.}
\label{tab:xai}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcccccc@{}}
\toprule
Model $\cdot$ LOO & Comp & Suff & Gap & AOPC-C & AOPC-S & Plaus. \\
\midrule
Classical & 0.125 & 0.030 & $+$0.096 & 0.150 & 0.011 & 0.75 \\
BiLSTM    & 0.270 & 0.120 & $+$0.257 & \textbf{0.289} & 0.064 & 0.69 \\
Encoder   & 0.162 & 0.033 & $+$0.135 & 0.193 & 0.007 & 0.68 \\
LLM       & 0.083 & 0.420 & $+$0.047 & 0.126 & 0.359 & 0.63 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

We find that:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{LOO is faithful for every pillar}: the gap over random removal is positive in all four
  cases (Figure~\ref{fig:faith});
  \item it is \textbf{strongest for the BiLSTM} (gap $+0.257$) and \textbf{weakest for the LLM} (gap
  $+0.047$); the LLM's high sufficiency (0.42) means its highlights are the least sharp;
  \item the same method works on the non-differentiable SVR, the BiLSTM, the encoder, and the LLM, so
  no gradients or model internals are needed;
  \item all four are also reasonably \emph{plausible} (reference overlap 0.63 to 0.75,
  Figure~\ref{fig:faithplaus}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Robustness to Unseen Questions}
\label{sec:leakage}
Scores collapse on questions the model has not seen before (RQ6). The random split shares questions
between train and test, because there are only 41 questions. Re-running the classical champion under a
question-held-out split (no shared questions), over five seeds, QWK \textbf{falls from 0.759 to 0.354}
(Table~\ref{tab:leakage}, Figure~\ref{fig:leakage}). Most of the seen-question performance was
question-specific memorisation, not transferable grading skill. With about six questions per
unseen-question test set the estimate is noisy ($\pm0.11$), but the drop is clear.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
Putting the results together, the ``best model'' question is genuinely three-dimensional, and each axis
behaves differently:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{Research agreement (QWK):} the four pillars are comparable.
  \item \textbf{Deployment (exact mark):} the fine-tuned LLM wins clearly.
  \item \textbf{Explainability:} LOO is faithful for every model, and most faithful for the small
  BiLSTM, not the largest model.
\end{itemize}

We can also place the numbers against the Arabic anchor~\citep{alaoui2024} in two careful ways. First,
on the \emph{same} unweighted Cohen's $\kappa$, our models score 0.47 (classical), about 0.62 (encoder
and RNN), and 0.71 (LLM), versus their 0.48 (BERT) and 0.60 (best transformer); this is context, not a
leaderboard, because the datasets differ. Second, and more fairly, the train/test \emph{generalisation
gap}: their best transformer over-fits by $+18.5$ points (95.67/77.22 accuracy), whereas our deep cells
are far tighter ($+1.4$ for the GTE encoder; $+3.0$ for Qwen, 83.3/80.3), and even the classical model
($+15.6$, 78.6/63.0) stays under their transformer. There is no hidden over-fitting beneath our
numbers.

For a classroom, the implication is concrete: a cheap classical model that explains itself faithfully
is an attractive default; the LLM is the right choice when the exact mark matters; and in both cases
the explanation shown to a teacher should be one that has been \emph{measured} faithful. The leakage
result is the strongest caution: until a question-held-out benchmark exists, reported Khmer ASAG
scores, including ours, should be read as seen-question performance.
